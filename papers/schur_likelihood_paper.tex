\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We introduce the \emph{Schur pseudo-likelihood}, a one-parameter family that damps the cross-block
coupling of the Gaussian likelihood through its Schur complements, and use it to estimate and evaluate
correlation matrices in high dimensions. The optimal damping has a closed form---the reliability of the
coupling, a James--Stein shrinkage---and the empirically optimal setting tracks it, improving
correlation recovery in the undersampled regime where the plain inverse is fragile.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
A correlation matrix is the hard part of a covariance matrix. Variances are a one-dimensional,
per-series estimation problem; the high-dimensional difficulty---the near-singular spectrum and the
unstable inverse that govern portfolio weights \citep{markowitz1952}, Gaussian and graphical models,
and out-of-sample scoring---lives entirely in the correlation. Engle's dynamic conditional correlation
separates the two for exactly this reason, and we follow suit, working on the correlation matrix $R$
directly.

The standard yardstick for a correlation estimate $\Rhat$ is the Gaussian log-likelihood it assigns to
held-out data, $\ell(\Rhat) = -\tfrac12(\logdet\Rhat + \tr(\Rhat^{-1}S))$ for a test correlation $S$.
Both terms are dominated by the smallest eigenvalues of $\Rhat$: $\logdet\Rhat=\sum_i\log\lambda_i$
diverges as any $\lambda_i\to0$, and $\Rhat^{-1}$ diverges with it. When the dimension $p$ is
comparable to the sample size $n$ those eigenvalues are unidentifiable (the Mar\v{c}enko--Pastur
regime), so both the likelihood and the minimum-variance portfolio $w\propto\Rhat^{-1}\mathbf1$ are
governed by the least reliable part of the spectrum \citep{ledoit2004,bun2017}. The remedy is to
discount the raw inverse. We do so continuously, through a single interpretable parameter that damps
the cross-block coupling, and show that its optimal value is the statistical reliability of that
coupling.

\section{The Schur pseudo-likelihood}
Partition the variables into $K$ ordered blocks. The Gaussian density factors exactly into block
conditionals, $p(x)=\prod_k p(x_k\mid x_{<k})$, each Gaussian with covariance the Schur complement
\[
  S_k \;=\; R_{kk} - R_{k,<k}\,R_{<k,<k}^{-1}\,R_{<k,k},
  \qquad
  \mu_{k\mid<k} = R_{k,<k}R_{<k,<k}^{-1}x_{<k},
\]
so that $\logdet R=\sum_k\logdet S_k$ and the likelihood is a sum of block-conditional terms, each
inverting only the conditioning block \citep{vecchia1988,katzfuss2021}. We damp the coupling---the
Schur complement and its associated regression---by a parameter $\gamma\in[0,1]$,
\[
  S_k(\gamma) = (1-\gamma)\,R_{kk} + \gamma\,S_k,
  \qquad
  \mu_{k\mid<k}(\gamma) = \gamma\,R_{k,<k}R_{<k,<k}^{-1}x_{<k},
\]
and define the score $\ell_\gamma(R;x)=\sum_k\log\mathcal N(x_k;\mu_{k\mid<k}(\gamma),S_k(\gamma))$. At
$\gamma=1$ this is the exact joint likelihood; at $\gamma=0$ each block is scored under its
unconditional correlation with no reference to the others---the block-diagonal composite likelihood
\citep{besag1975,lindsay1988,varin2011}. Intermediate $\gamma$ inverts no block larger than the
conditioning set and is better conditioned than the full likelihood. The same $\gamma$ interpolates an
allocation rule, from hierarchical risk parity at $\gamma=0$ \citep{lopezdeprado2016} to the
minimum-variance portfolio at $\gamma=1$ \citep{cotton2024}: damping the evaluation and damping the
allocation are one control.

\section{The optimal damping is the coupling reliability}
In a tractable two-block model---$x_1\sim\mathcal N(0,a)$ and $x_2=bx_1+\varepsilon$ with conditional
$R^2$ equal to $\rho^2=b^2a/(b^2a+s)$---the damped estimate uses the shrunk coupling $\gamma\hat b$ from
the ordinary least-squares estimate $\hat b$. Minimising the expected predictive residual gives a
closed form,
\[
  \gamma^\star \;=\; \frac{b^2}{b^2+\operatorname{Var}(\hat b)}
            \;=\; \frac{(n-2)\,\rho^2}{(n-2)\,\rho^2+(1-\rho^2)},
\]
the \emph{reliability} of the estimated coupling: a Wiener/James--Stein shrinkage
\citep{james1961,ledoit2012}. It tends to $1$ as $n\to\infty$ or $\rho^2\to1$ (the coupling is
trustworthy; use the full likelihood) and to $0$ as $\rho^2\to0$ (the coupling is noise; decouple),
and is interior when the coupling is partially reliable. The maximiser of $\ell_\gamma$ recovers the
true predictive law for every $\gamma$ while implying an inflated correlation, so $\ell_\gamma$ is a
scoring and regularisation device, not a free estimation objective.

\section{Correlation recovery}
We draw true correlation matrices with a two-level block structure---within-block correlation fixed,
between-block correlation $v$, the coupling the Schur complement damps---and sweep $v$ against the
aspect ratio $n/p$ on a grid dense near unity, $200$ replications at $p=128$. For each cell we locate
the $\gamma$ minimising off-diagonal recovery error.

The optimal $\gamma^\star(v,n/p)$ (Figure~\ref{fig:gamma}) is essentially a function of the coupling
strength: $\gamma^\star\approx0$ at $v=0$, $\approx\tfrac12$ at $v=0.1$, and $\approx1$ for
$v\ge0.45$, dipping where samples are scarcest ($n/p=0.6$). This is the reliability law of the previous
section, recovered across the plane. Ranking estimators by recovery error (Figure~\ref{fig:win}),
interior-$\gamma$ Schur is best in the weak-coupling ($v\le0.2$), undersampled ($n/p\lesssim1.1$)
quadrant; the empirical correlation wins once well sampled, and shrinkage wins the strong-coupling
undersampled corner. A paired bootstrap over the replications confirms every Schur-winning cell at the
$95\%$ level. The margins are largest at $v=0$, where damping exploits a coupling that is genuinely
absent, and modest but significant for interior $\gamma$; the durable conclusion is the law, not the
size of the edge.

\section{Real data: recovering a proxy correlation}
A correlation matrix has no observable truth, so a real-data test must choose a surrogate; we choose a
recovery target rather than an out-of-sample-likelihood proxy, since the latter measures density, not
recovery. Within each window we treat the correlation of \emph{hourly} returns (hundreds of
observations) as a high-accuracy proxy for the latent correlation, and ask whether estimators fed only
the window's \emph{daily} returns---the undersampled regime---recover it. Over $61$ weekly and monthly
windows of $51$ liquid crypto assets, with many random sub-universes spanning aspect ratios
$n/p\in[0.15,3.8]$, the interior-damped Schur estimate ($\gamma\approx0.5$) recovers the proxy
correlation best at every aspect ratio (Fig.~\ref{fig:realized}). Under a window-clustered bootstrap it
significantly outperforms the best shrinkage estimator (OAS, $\Delta\text{RMSE}=0.027$, $95\%$ CI
$[0.019,0.036]$) and, by larger margins, both endpoints---the full likelihood / empirical correlation
($\gamma=1$) and full decoupling ($\gamma=0$); it is the single best estimator in $57\%$ of windows.
The optimal damping is interior and stable, $\gamma^\star\approx0.5$, as on synthetic data. The same
estimator was \emph{not} best under an out-of-sample-likelihood criterion, a concrete instance of the
recurring theme that the verdict follows the target. (Hourly sampling carries mild Epps attenuation,
and crypto correlations are unusually strong; we report the recovery comparison, not a trading result.)

\section{The matrix versus its inverse}
The optimal damping inverts with the target. Recovering the correlation matrix wants large $\gamma$
(trust the coupling); recovering its inverse---the partial correlations, the graphical structure---wants
small $\gamma$, because the inverse amplifies coupling noise. The same estimate is near-best for the
matrix and near-worst for its inverse. Inverse recovery is also structure-dependent: a
block/equicorrelation truth has a near-diagonal precision (partial correlations of order $1/p$), so the
zero-correlation baseline is unbeatable there for trivial reasons, whereas on truths with genuine graph
structure estimation wins. On locally coupled, AR$(1)$-type precision the Schur estimate at
$\gamma\approx0.75$ is the best edge-recovery rule at every coupling strength; on arbitrary sparse
precision plain shrinkage wins, since contiguous-block damping cannot match an arbitrary graph. The
recurring lesson is that ``best'' is undefined until the target is, and good scoring rules---the
inversion-light variogram score for matrix recovery, the likelihood for density \citep{gneiting2007,
scheuerer2015}---must be matched to it.

\section{Conclusion}
The Schur pseudo-likelihood is a one-knob interpolation between decoupled and full Gaussian scoring
whose optimal setting is the closed-form reliability of the cross-block coupling. On correlation
matrices in high dimensions---the regime where the plain inverse is fragile---the empirically best
damping tracks that reliability, and the interpolant improves recovery in the undersampled regime and,
for locally structured precision, the recovery of the inverse. It is a regularised, better-conditioned
likelihood for estimating and evaluating correlation when the dimension rivals the sample size.
